QQQ vs SCHD: Which ETF Is Better for Long-Term Investors?

QQQ has delivered extraordinary growth by tracking the Nasdaq-100. SCHD has built steady wealth through quality dividend stocks. Both are among the most popular ETFs in America — but they serve fundamentally different purposes. Here's what the data actually shows.

By DivSprout · May 29, 2026

The Core Difference

QQQ (Invesco QQQ Trust) tracks the Nasdaq-100 Index — the 100 largest non-financial companies listed on the Nasdaq exchange. In practice, that means a heavy concentration in technology: Apple, Microsoft, Nvidia, Amazon, Meta, Alphabet, and Tesla together make up roughly 40–45% of the entire fund. QQQ does not screen for dividends, profitability ratios, or income. It simply buys the largest companies on the Nasdaq.

SCHD (Schwab U.S. Dividend Equity ETF) tracks the Dow Jones U.S. Dividend 100 Index — a rules-based index that screens for companies with at least 10 consecutive years of paying dividends, strong free cash flow relative to total debt, solid return on equity, and above-average dividend yield. The result is a portfolio of financially healthy, mature businesses that tend to sit in sectors like financials, healthcare, consumer staples, energy, and industrials.

These are not two versions of the same thing. They are built on different philosophies: QQQ bets on growth, SCHD bets on income and quality. Understanding which one fits your situation requires looking honestly at the data — not just cherry-picking a favorable time period.

Historical Returns: What the Data Shows

QQQ has outperformed SCHD on a total return basis over most long periods. That is simply true, and it would be misleading to pretend otherwise. The Nasdaq-100 has been one of the best-performing major indices in the world over the past 15 years, driven by the explosive growth of companies like Apple, Microsoft, and Nvidia.

However, return data without context is dangerous. QQQ's long-term outperformance came alongside brutal drawdowns that many investors could not — or did not — survive emotionally. Selling at the bottom of a correction because you couldn't stomach the losses turns paper underperformance into real permanent losses.

The pattern is clear: QQQ wins most years in a bull market, often by a large margin. But when conditions turn — like 2022 — SCHD's defensive characteristics shine. SCHD also outperformed in 2016 and 2021, years when value and dividend stocks were in favour.

Over the full period from SCHD's inception in late 2011 to end of 2024, QQQ delivered approximately 18–20% CAGR (compound annual growth rate), compared to SCHD's approximately 12–14% CAGR — both with dividends reinvested. That gap is real and significant when compounded over many years.

The Dot-Com Warning: Why QQQ's History Matters

QQQ launched in March 1999 — right near the peak of the dot-com bubble. From its peak in March 2000 to its trough in October 2002, QQQ fell approximately 83%. It did not recover to its year-2000 peak until 2017 — 17 years later.

This is not ancient history to be dismissed. It is a real data point about what happens to technology-heavy growth funds when sentiment reverses and valuations compress. SCHD did not exist during that period, but a dividend-focused portfolio of quality companies with strong cash flows weathered the dot-com crash significantly better than the Nasdaq-100.

The lesson is not that QQQ is bad — it has delivered exceptional returns over the right holding periods. The lesson is that QQQ requires a very long time horizon and the emotional discipline to hold through catastrophic drawdowns without selling.

Dividend Income: A Different Way to Measure Returns

For investors who care about income — not just portfolio value — SCHD operates in a completely different league. QQQ's dividend yield of roughly 0.5–0.6% is essentially noise. On a $100,000 investment, that's about $500–$600 per year before tax. Most QQQ investors do not rely on dividends at all; they plan to sell shares to generate income.

SCHD, by contrast, has grown its dividend per share from approximately $0.40 annually at inception in 2011 to around $2.60–$2.80 annually by 2024 — a compound annual growth rate of roughly 11–12%. On a $100,000 initial investment in SCHD held since inception with dividends reinvested, the annual dividend income received today would be substantially higher than 3.5% on the original cost basis — a concept known as yield on cost.

Critically, SCHD did not cut its dividend during the 2022 bear market. It raised it. That consistency — raising dividends during down markets — is what separates quality dividend funds from high-yield traps. Risk and Volatility: The Numbers Behind the Gut Feel

Beta measures how much a fund moves relative to the broader market. QQQ's beta is approximately 1.2 — meaning it tends to move about 20% more than the S&P 500 in either direction. When the market falls 10%, QQQ historically falls around 12%. When it rises 10%, QQQ tends to rise about 12%.

SCHD's beta is approximately 0.7 — it moves about 30% less than the market. In a 10% market decline, SCHD typically falls around 7%. This lower volatility is not an accident. SCHD screens for dividend sustainability, which filters toward companies with strong balance sheets, stable earnings, and predictable cash flows. These characteristics naturally produce lower price swings.

For investors who are retired, near retirement, or simply know they cannot psychologically tolerate watching their portfolio fall 30–40% in a bad year, SCHD's volatility profile is a genuine advantage — not just a consolation prize.

Sector Concentration: QQQ's Biggest Hidden Risk

As of 2025–2026, technology companies make up over 50% of QQQ. Add communication services (which includes Google and Meta) and the concentration rises further. The ten largest holdings in QQQ — Apple, Microsoft, Nvidia, Amazon, Meta, Alphabet (Class A), Alphabet (Class C), Tesla, Broadcom, and Costco — together account for roughly 50–55% of the fund.

This concentration creates a specific risk: if the technology sector falls out of favour due to regulatory action, rising interest rates, or a reversal in AI investment sentiment, QQQ gets hit far harder than a diversified fund. The 2022 experience — when rising rates particularly punished high-multiple growth stocks — was a preview of this dynamic.

SCHD is meaningfully more diversified. No single sector exceeds roughly 20–25% of the fund. Financials, healthcare, consumer staples, industrials, and energy each have substantial representation. Importantly, QQQ excludes all financial sector companies by rule (the Nasdaq-100 index does not include financial firms), which means no banks, no insurance companies, and no diversified financial services in QQQ's portfolio.

Tax Efficiency: Both Are Surprisingly Similar Here

Unlike JEPI, which generates significant ordinary income through options premiums, both QQQ and SCHD are relatively tax-efficient. SCHD's dividends are predominantly qualified dividends — taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on your income bracket, rather than at ordinary income rates.

QQQ distributes very little in dividends (yield ~0.5%), so the tax drag from distributions is minimal. Most QQQ investors are subject to tax only when they sell shares — and if they hold long enough, those gains are taxed at the long-term capital gains rate.

The tax difference between QQQ and SCHD is therefore not as stark as the difference between SCHD and JEPI. Both are reasonable choices for taxable accounts. However, SCHD's higher yield does mean more taxable income each year, which is worth factoring in for investors in high income tax brackets who are still accumulating wealth.

Expense Ratio: SCHD Wins Clearly

SCHD's expense ratio of 0.06% per year is one of the lowest among broadly diversified equity ETFs. QQQ charges 0.20% — more than three times as much. On a $100,000 portfolio, that's $60/year versus $200/year.

Over 30 years of compounding, the cost difference becomes more meaningful. Assuming 10% annual growth before fees, $100,000 invested in SCHD grows to roughly $1,670,000 after 30 years at 0.06% fees. At QQQ's 0.20% fee on the same 10% baseline, the ending value is approximately $1,630,000. The cost gap alone accounts for roughly $40,000 in final wealth — not enormous, but not nothing.

It is worth noting that Invesco also offers QQQM — an ETF tracking the same Nasdaq-100 index as QQQ but with a lower expense ratio of 0.15%, specifically designed for long-term buy-and-hold investors. For most individual investors without institutional liquidity needs, QQQM is the more cost-effective choice over QQQ.

The "Sell Shares vs. Collect Dividends" Debate

One of the most persistent debates in personal finance is whether it's better to invest in growth funds like QQQ and sell shares periodically to generate income — the "total return" approach — or to invest in dividend funds like SCHD and live off the income without selling.

In pure mathematical terms, the total return approach often wins on paper, especially during strong bull markets. Selling 3–4% of a larger portfolio can generate more cash than collecting 3–4% dividends from a smaller one.

But mathematics does not account for human psychology. Several real-world complications favour dividend investing for many people:

  • Sequence of returns risk. If you retire and immediately encounter a 30–40% bear market, selling shares to fund living expenses depletes your portfolio at exactly the wrong moment — permanently reducing its recovery potential. Dividend income arrives regardless of share price.
  • Behavioural discipline. It is psychologically much easier to avoid selling dividend stocks during a crash (the income keeps arriving) than to execute a planned withdrawal strategy when your portfolio is down 35%.
  • Forced selling. Living off dividends means never having to sell. Living off capital gains requires continuous decision-making about timing, tax lots, and portfolio rebalancing.

For investors in the accumulation phase with decades ahead, QQQ's growth potential is compelling. For investors near or in retirement who rely on their portfolio for income, SCHD's dividend reliability has real practical advantages that do not show up in a simple return comparison. The Case for Holding Both

QQQ and SCHD complement each other effectively, and many investors hold both for exactly that reason. QQQ provides growth exposure to the technology companies that have driven markets over the last two decades. SCHD provides a foundation of dividend income, lower volatility, and sector diversification that technology-heavy funds lack.

A simple blended portfolio might look like: 60% QQQ for growth, 40% SCHD for income and stability. A more income-oriented investor closer to retirement might flip that: 30% QQQ, 70% SCHD. There is no single right answer — it depends entirely on your time horizon, income needs, and tolerance for portfolio swings.

What matters is understanding what each fund actually does — not buying based on past returns alone. QQQ in 2020 returned +48.6%. That same year, SCHD returned +11.8%. Extrapolating that gap forward would have led you to dramatically overestimate QQQ's sustained advantage.
