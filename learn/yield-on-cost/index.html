Yield on Cost (YOC)
Explained

Educational content only. This article is for informational purposes only and does not constitute investment advice. Consult a qualified financial adviser before making investment decisions.

Yield on cost (YOC) measures how much dividend income you're earning relative to what you originally paid for your shares — not their current market price. It's a metric used almost exclusively by long-term, buy-and-hold dividend investors to track how their income has grown over time.

The formula

Yield on Cost = (Current Annual Dividend Per Share ÷ Your Purchase Price Per Share) × 100

The key difference from standard dividend yield is the denominator: yield on cost uses your cost basis, not today's market price.

A concrete example

You buy shares of a dividend-growth company in 2015 at $40 per share. At the time, it pays $1.20 per share annually — a yield of 3%. Over the next decade the company steadily raises its dividend. By 2025, it's paying $2.80 per share annually. The stock now trades at $90, so its current yield on that price is about 3.1% — roughly the same as when you bought it.

But your yield on cost is very different:

Current annual dividend $2.80 / share
Your original purchase price $40.00 / share
Your yield on cost 7.0%
You're earning 7% on your original investment, even though the stock officially yields ~3% to anyone buying today.

A new investor buying this stock today gets 3.1% yield. You've been compounding for a decade and your effective income rate is 7%. This gap widens further if dividends keep growing.

Why YOC matters — and where it misleads

YOC is a useful internal scorecard. It tells you whether a long-term holding is delivering the income growth you expected when you bought it. A rising YOC over time is a strong signal that a dividend-growth strategy is working as intended.

However, it can also create a cognitive trap: an investor might hold a deteriorating company partly because their "YOC is 12%" — which sounds impressive — when in reality the business fundamentals have weakened and a dividend cut is coming. YOC reflects the past; it doesn't predict the future.

It's also worth noting that YOC is entirely personal — it means nothing to anyone who didn't buy at your specific price. It's not a useful comparison metric between stocks; it's a metric about your relationship with a specific investment.

YOC and DRIP compounding together

When you combine a DRIP with a dividend-growth stock, YOC grows even faster. Each reinvested dividend buys more shares at the prevailing price. Those shares then receive future (higher) dividends. The effective yield on your total cost basis grows not just because the dividend is rising, but because you're accumulating more shares with each reinvestment cycle.

This is why the DRIP breakdown table in DivSprout's calculator shows YOC for each year — so you can see how rapidly your personal income rate grows relative to what you originally invested, as both the dividend and your share count increase over time.

How to think about a "good" YOC

There's no universal benchmark — it depends entirely on how long you've held the investment, what the starting yield was, and how fast dividends have grown. As a rough frame: if you bought a stock at a 3–4% yield and hold it for 10–15 years with a company growing dividends at 6–8% per year, a YOC of 7–10% or more is achievable. YOC in the double digits over very long holding periods is common in well-known dividend growth stories.
